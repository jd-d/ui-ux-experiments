Global Freight Indices Slump on Weak Demand

Trigger risk: Watch Trade Slowdown SHIPPING Confidence: High Score: 50

What’s happening

Global shipping markets have softened significantly, reflecting a broader cooldown in goods trade. In early 2025, container freight rates plunged – the key Shanghai Export Container Index fell by about 40% between January and March. Other freight measures (like bulk shipping rates) also dropped, indicating excess vessel capacity and weaker demand for physical goods. By mid-2025, there were early signs of stabilization – UNCTAD reports global trade volumes edged up roughly 1–2% in the first half – but the momentum is much weaker than the post-pandemic boom.

Why it matters

  • Recession signal: Slumping freight prices often presage slowdowns in manufacturing and commodity demand. The current slump has raised eyebrows that a “hard landing” or recession in some economies could be looming. For example, fewer container shipments from Asia to the West align with retailers reducing inventories amid uncertain consumer demand.
  • Relief on inflation: A silver lining is that cheaper freight costs ease supply chain pressures and could help lower consumer price inflation (unlike 2021–22 when high shipping costs were adding to prices). However, if driven by demand weakness, it’s a sign of a cooling economy.
  • Panama Canal constraints: On top of demand factors, supply-side disruptions like the Panama Canal drought have impacted shipping. In 2023 and mid-2025, Panama imposed draft and transit limits due to low water levels, cutting daily transits to ~32 from the usual 36. This created backlogs of ships waiting (100+ vessels at peaks) and forced some shippers to reroute or lighten loads. Climate-related disruptions of major chokepoints can exacerbate volatility in freight rates.

Watch next

  • Peak season volumes: The late-2025 holiday shipping season will be a litmus test. If retailers’ orders remain soft and freight indices don’t uptick as usual in Q4, it suggests enduring weakness into 2026.
  • Idle fleet and new ships: Monitor the percentage of the container ship fleet that is idle. It’s creeping up as demand lags and new, more efficient megaships are being delivered. An idle fleet above ~10–15% would indicate significant overcapacity.
  • Geopolitical wildcards: Any resolution or escalation in conflicts (e.g. Ukraine grain corridor, tensions in East Asia) could swiftly alter trade flows and shipping demand. Also, policy shifts like export controls or tariffs (US–China, etc.) can tug global trade volumes up or down.
Sources: UNCTAD; UNCTAD News; Seatrade Maritime
