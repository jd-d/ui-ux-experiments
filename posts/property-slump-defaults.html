23 September 2025

China Property Crisis Deepens with Developer Defaults

Trigger risk: Elevated Property Bust CHINA CREDIT Confidence: High Score: 65

Latest developments

China’s real estate sector – once a key engine of growth – is in protracted crisis. Dozens of property developers have defaulted on bonds and loans since 2021. Evergrande, which defaulted in 2021, was formally ordered into liquidation in 2023, and others like Country Garden (formerly China’s largest developer) are teetering with bond extensions and restructuring proposals. New home sales continue to languish – official data shows 2024 year-to-date home sales are down by double digits (around 14% lower than the prior year).

Why it matters

  • Financial contagion risk: Chinese developers amassed hundreds of billions in debt during the boom. As they default, banks, trust funds, and investors (including offshore bondholders) face losses. So far, China’s government has managed this through controlled restructurings, but a disorderly collapse of a giant developer could spook markets globally.
  • Economic drag: The property sector (housing, construction, real estate services) makes up an estimated 25–30% of China’s GDP. The downturn has led to construction halts and layoffs. It’s also hit local government finances hard – much of their revenue came from land sales to developers, which have now dried up. This amplifies the concurrent local debt issues (many local financing vehicles are also in trouble).
  • Social stability: Prolonged property woes erode household wealth and confidence. Urban Chinese households have a large share of wealth in housing. Evergrande’s collapse, for example, left many homebuyers with paid-for but unfinished apartments, sparking protests. The government is keen to avoid these issues boiling over.

Watch next

  • Policy support: Beijing has been rolling out piecemeal support (rate cuts, easing home purchase restrictions in big cities, bailout funds for some projects). The effectiveness of these measures in stabilizing home prices will be crucial. Any signal of a broad bailout (or conversely, decision to let big firms fail) will shape outcomes.
  • Developer endgames: Pay attention to how Country Garden’s restructuring unfolds and if other major developers like Sunac or Shimao follow similar paths. If one of the systemically important developers were to enter a chaotic bankruptcy, that would raise this risk to critical.
  • Market sentiment: Signs of homebuyer return – e.g. rising housing transaction volumes or a bottoming of prices – would indicate the worst may be over. Conversely, continued price declines or protests (like the 2022 mortgage boycott movement by frustrated buyers) would signal deeper trouble.
Sources: YIP Institute; SCMP
