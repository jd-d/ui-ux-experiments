Panama Canal Drought Restricts Shipping Traffic

Trigger risk: Elevated Climate Disruption SHIPPING Confidence: High Score: 60

What’s new

An ongoing drought has significantly constrained the Panama Canal, one of the world’s key maritime trade arteries. Canal authorities have repeatedly restricted vessel draft (the depth of ships) and capped daily transits to conserve water. In mid-2025, for instance, the maximum draft allowed was about 13.4 m (44 feet), meaning many larger ships had to either lighten their loads or take alternate routes. Furthermore, only 32 ships were allowed through per day (down from the normal 36), causing backups of dozens of vessels waiting at each end.

Why it matters

  • Supply chain impact: The Panama Canal handles ~5% of global trade. Restrictions there ripple through supply chains – shipments from Asia to the US East Coast, for example, face delays or higher costs if ships must detour via Suez or Cape Horn. In late 2023 and mid-2025, backlog peaked over 100 ships waiting, delaying goods ranging from electronics to grains.
  • Climate as a systemic risk: This disruption underscores how climate change can impose a systemic economic risk. The drought in Panama (linked to shifting weather patterns and possibly exacerbated by El Niño) lowered Gatun Lake’s water levels drastically, impairing canal operations. It’s a stark reminder that critical infrastructure built for past climate norms may struggle under new extremes.
  • Upgrades and alternatives: The Panama Canal Authority is exploring long-term solutions (like new reservoirs or water-conservation measures) to bolster resilience. In the meantime, shippers are hedging by using other routes or deploying smaller vessels. Some are even advancing timetables for Arctic routes in summer as an alternative – though that’s not yet a major factor.

Watch next

  • Hydrological forecasts: The severity of the upcoming dry season (late 2025 into 2026) will dictate if further restrictions or complete closures occur. If El Niño conditions continue, expect strict transit limits to persist or tighten.
  • Global shipping rates: Any prolonged Canal bottleneck tends to raise freight rates for certain routes (especially US East/Gulf Coast). If rates spike in those markets, it indicates the Canal constraints are biting again.
  • Infrastructure responses: Keep an eye on announcements from the Panama Canal Authority – e.g. plans for dredging deeper channels or accelerating water management projects. Funding and executing major infrastructure changes will be challenging but critical if these droughts become frequent.
Sources: SupplyChains Magazine; Lloyd’s List
